Week 1: Making a Website

For the first week's assignment, our task was to curate our own website for our work.


To begin, I first needed to grasp the concepts of HTML and CSS.

HTML is the language for designing what is seen on the web browser.

It goes hand and hand with the other digital language of CSS.


I started out by installing a few different items first: Visual Studio code,

Git via my terminal, and the brackets extension. Next up was setting up the repository

for my website through GitHub Pages. Git allows for the creation of an empty repository.

After some use of terminal, it was set up and ready for the next step.



Setting up the index.html took some time. It involved the uses of committing, staging changes,

and pushing functions. After a change is made to the code, you press save. Next, I went over to the changes

section and staged them. Then, I had to commit them. To do so, I had to type any command into the message bar,

then press commit. Pushing is a function on VSC that allows the user to make the changes go live. Pulling on the

other hand,takes those changes back and removing them from the live version of the site. Cloning is another function

which allows you to make copies of selected elements within the html code. Had a little bit of diffculty

navigating through this step, but was able to figure it out in the end.



Moving along, the next step was finding a good template to use. I went on to the W3 school's website and found this one.



I made changes to the template via the Tryit Editor on W3's site until adjusted them to my liking.

It worked effectively at letting me see instant changes to what the site would appear like,

rather than having to push out each individual change on VSC and waiting for the site to update it.



I added an About Me page, Weekly Assignment Pages, Final Assignment page, as well as a Contact section.



Finally, I copied the template code and pasted it into my index.html file on VSC. Now just time to commit

and push to see all the changees go live!



As you can see, I was able to get my website up and running smoothly. The process wasn't the easiest, but the outcome was

all worth it in the end. My own site, pretty cool!



Week 2: CNC Routing

Week two gave us light to the first hands on assignment in the course.

Before beginning this assignment, I received training to use the handheld cutter.


First, I went onto Autodesk fusion 360. This was our first assignment involving

the use of the application. It is a CAD/CAM software used for product design/development.

Here, I was able to design the sketch for my personalized organizer.



After the sketch was complete, I went into the render mode

to alter the appearance of my piece. In this mode, I was able to change the material you are

using (mahogany, cherry, pine wood, etc.) and much more.



Then, I switched on over to Adobe Illustrator. The main goal here was to prepare

an .svg file for the shaper to cut. I pasted an image in of the shaper origin illustrator

guide to help me identify the colors corresponding to each cut. Example: Black = Exterior cut,

Gray = Pocket cut, etc. Now, I am ready to use the shaper and begin the cutting!



To start using the shaper, I followed this list of steps:


1. Vacuum the surface.

2. Double-side tape the back. (No double-sided tape available, used 4 screws.)

3. Drill holes and then screws into corners secure board into place (Change screwhead

first and it is crucial that crew is below surface material)

4. Insert the correct endmill by twisting the rod to loosen it from the shaper.

5. Using a wrench, twist and turn to the left on the endmill to loosen it and change the

spindle screw.

6. Put the new screw into spindle down at least 2/3’s into the endmill. Next, holding down

button on it, twist to the right with wrench to secure it back on (let off button when it gets

stiff to turn).

7. Place the endmill back onto the shaper, attach the cord back and twist the rod to secure it

back on.

8. Insert the USB into the shaper.

9. Turn the shaper on, start a new scan, import and place design.

10. Create cut settings -> 0.25in -> Pocket Engrave -> 0.25in Z touch

11. Make sure the vacuum is connected to shaper before turning on spindle and making cuts.


The cutting process took some time, but I reached a final product that I was happy with.

I unscrewed the screws from my personalized organizer, retrieved it from the station, sanded

up the edges, and then vacuumed everything up. The piece was complete!




On some side notes, there were a few things I wish to have fixed/changed for my organizer.

I wanted to keep my edges filleted on my holder, but unfortunately was unable to do so due to the placement of the screws

(No double-sided tape left, so I had to screw each corner down). Also, the pocket cuts could of been a little bit more precise

and free of the ridges you see. Besides these minor inconveniences, I am happy with the final product. As you can see, it holds

a phone, pens, etc. quite nicely! The circle and the way my initials came out make for a nice finish. I plan to fix the pocket cuts

and potenitally cut out the circle into a pcoket cut as well.

Week 3: FDM Printing

For week 3, our assignment was to 3D print a pot which is able to fit in our personalized holder.

This was my first assignment involving the 3D printers, so I had to receive the training to do so prior.


The first step in the process for designing a pot was to measure the diameter of the circle in my holder,

which was roughly 3 inches. This is to make sure that my pot will fit within my CNC holder. To start the design,

I went opened up the Fusion 360 application and began to follow along the tutorial on our class website for creating

a pot.



During this process, I had to design sketches, use the extrude and fillet features, and more. I started my pot by

using the circular pattern tool and adjusted the circumference. Next, I extruded the center of the pot until it was at a

good enough height. I also ended up and added supports because my pot extruded outward. After I finished designing the pot's

design in Fusion, I exported it as an .stl file.



Now, it is almost time to print out my pot. I went over to the fiber lab, powered up one of the Macs, and opened up the PrusaSlicer

application to view my print.



Using this application, it let me view my design layer by layer to make sure it would print correctly. After this was done, I exported

it as a G-code to an SD card for the MK3 Mini printer to read. Next, I waited for the printer to warm up and then loaded on silver filament.

From here, I then selected the file that I want it to print. As required, I waited 30 minutes by the printer to ensure that is working properly.




Here is the final result. It took a total of 15 hours to print out my pot. It has a nice silver finish and fits with my CNC Holder nicely.

Week 4: SLA Printing

SLA printing, also known as Stereolithography and resin printing, is a

certain method of 3Dprinting that uses a light source to turn liquid resin into a hard plastic.


I had the choice between 3 designs with tutorials that were offered, and

I chose the one for the spinning ring. After deciding on the spinning design, I opened

up Fusion 360 to begin following along with the tutorial for the ring.



The video tutorial for the spinning ring was quite fast and often difficult to follow at some points, so I slowed

down the playback speed to help keep me at pace. I learned how to use some new tools in Fusion


After I was finished in Fusion, I exported it as an .stl file and then put in

our Google drive folder for it to be printed out. Due to the hazards of the process (resin

leaks causing skin rashes, irratation, etc.), there are no trainings for using the machine and

I had to detach it from the supports but it came off nicely. Unfortunately, (according to one of the TAs) it was unable to spin due to a layering issue

when being printed out. Nonetheless, it still makes for a cool and unique ring!

Week 5: Vinyl Cutting

By definition, digital fabrication involves a design process mixing together 3D modeling

or computer-assisted designs. The process of vinyl cutting uses of these elements together; creating a design

on a computer, sending it to a machine to be cut, and then placing what it cuts onto another object.


For my shirt’s design, I wanted it to be Pokémon themed. I decided to choose the ghost/poison

type pokemon Gengar. Ever since I was a kid, I have always had an obsession to the brand and its iconic

characters. My friends and I even started playing Pokemon Go again here on campus recently. It’s been a

really fun way to pass the time!


I opened up Adobe Illustrator and created a new blank file. Next, I downloaded a png of Gengar,

which worked well due to its transparent background. I had to do an image trace—>expand to make it its own object on the

file. A vector image is an image that is defined by mathematical terms and connects its lines together to create shapes. A raster image

is a bitmap image that is viewable on most computer/electronic screens. The cutter uses images defined be these elements to create your cut.

Next, I used the text feature and typed out “Gotta catch ‘em all” in the classical Pokemon styled font. Once again, I image trace—>expand for

the text. I exported it as a png file and saved it to my flash drive.



After my file was complete, I headed over to the fiber lab to use the machine. I plugged in my flash drive

to upload my design onto the computer used for vinyl cutting. I pressed cut to send over my design to the cutter, and began

the next few steps of the process.


Before using the vinyl cutter, I first cut out a piece of apparel vinyl with the right proportions to fit my design,

and then loaded it on, making sure it’s clamped down securely.


Next, I set the speed and force on the cutter. With apparel vinyl, the speed stays at 12m/s and the force being at 93gs.

After the machine finished the cut, I removed the vinyl, threw away any leftover material, and begin peeling off the parts that

I would not need on the shirt.


Now, it was time to use the heat press to place my design on my shirt. Before beginning, I waited about 15 minutes for the heat press

to fully turn on/heat to rise up after switching on the power. Once it was ready, I placed down the shirt flatly and gave it an iron. I then

prepared my Pokemon design over the ironed shirt, making sure everything was nice and center.



Never touch the top of the press, place shirt down flatly and hold down for 5 seconds. After it was pressed, I peeled the remaining piece

of the sticky side of the vinyl.



My shirt is now complete and looks great!

Week 6: Laser Cutting

Welcome to Laser Cutting week!

To begin the assignment, I first had to choose from one of the box designs

on the generator website.



I chose the option for boxes with lids. Next, I changed the thickness of the box to around 3.20mm. After the template

was created, I saved it as an .svg file.



I then went on over and opened the file in Adobe Illustrator. Here, I added my initials onto the box and rastered

them as their own object so they don’t get cut through. It is now ready to be exported as in and then opened in for the cut.


I opened the tray beneath where the laser cutter is for the plywood needed to create my box. I used a piece of 1/8” birch plywood

from the drawer labeled “free use” and then scanned it to the cutter.


Before starting the cut, I turned on the air filter to lower the chances of a fire. When using the cutter, you must stay within the

rectangular area and keep good watch to prevent fires from occurring.



My design took around 8-10 minutes to be cut. Once the cutter was completed

with my design, I powered the it down and turned off the air filter. I removed the pieces

of my box from the tray and began assembling it together. Since I forgot to edit the lid settings

on the generator, I had to imrovise. I cut out an extra small square piece to be used as a cover over

\

my box. Here is the finished product.
